History of India-Pakistan Water Disputes

Introduction

Water has been a source of tension between India and Pakistan since their independence in 1947. The two countries share several major rivers, and their management has been a complex issue intertwined with broader geopolitical conflicts. This article examines the history of water disputes between these nuclear-armed neighbors, focusing on the Indus Waters Treaty and subsequent conflicts over water resources.

Partition and Early Water Conflicts

When the British partitioned the Indian subcontinent in 1947, the new international border cut across the Indus River system, creating immediate challenges for water management. The headwaters of major rivers that flowed into Pakistan were located in India, giving India potential control over Pakistan's water supply. This geographical reality created an immediate security concern for the newly formed Pakistan.

The first water crisis emerged in April 1948 when India stopped the flow of water in canals that crossed into Pakistan from the Indian state of East Punjab. While India eventually restored the flow, this incident highlighted Pakistan's vulnerability and the urgent need for a permanent water-sharing agreement.

The Indus Waters Treaty of 1960

After nearly a decade of negotiations facilitated by the World Bank, India and Pakistan signed the Indus Waters Treaty on September 19, 1960. The treaty was a landmark achievement in international water law and remains one of the most successful water-sharing agreements in the world.

The treaty allocated the eastern rivers (Ravi, Beas, and Sutlej) to India and the western rivers (Indus, Jhelum, and Chenab) to Pakistan. While India received approximately 20% of the total water in the Indus system, Pakistan secured rights to 80% of the water, reflecting its heavy dependence on these rivers for agriculture and other needs.

Importantly, the treaty established a permanent Indus Commission with commissioners from both countries and detailed mechanisms for sharing data, resolving disputes, and allowing limited use of the western rivers by India for non-consumptive purposes such as power generation.

Implementation and Challenges

The Indus Waters Treaty has survived three wars between India and Pakistan (1965, 1971, and 1999) and numerous periods of heightened tension. This durability is often cited as evidence of its success. However, implementation has not been without challenges.

Pakistan has frequently expressed concerns about India's construction of dams and hydroelectric projects on the western rivers, arguing that these projects could potentially give India the ability to manipulate water flow during times of conflict. India has maintained that its projects comply with the treaty's provisions for non-consumptive use.

The Baglihar Dam Controversy

One of the most significant disputes arose over India's construction of the Baglihar Dam on the Chenab River. In 2005, Pakistan approached the World Bank to appoint a neutral expert to resolve differences over the dam's design. The neutral expert, Raymond Lafitte, issued a verdict in 2007 that partially favored both countries, requiring some modifications to India's design while allowing the project to proceed.

The Baglihar case highlighted both the strengths and limitations of the treaty's dispute resolution mechanisms. While it demonstrated that the treaty could handle disagreements, it also revealed the growing tensions over water resources as both countries faced increasing demand due to population growth and economic development.

The Kishanganga Project Dispute

Another major dispute emerged over India's Kishanganga Hydroelectric Project on a tributary of the Jhelum River. Pakistan objected to the project, arguing that it would reduce water flow to its own planned Neelum-Jhelum Hydroelectric Project downstream.

In 2010, Pakistan took the case to the Permanent Court of Arbitration (PCA) in The Hague. In its 2013 verdict, the PCA allowed India to proceed with the project but required it to maintain a minimum flow of water to Pakistan. This case further tested the treaty's dispute resolution mechanisms and highlighted the growing complexity of water issues between the two countries.

Recent Tensions and Threats

In recent years, water disputes have become increasingly politicized and linked to broader security concerns. Following a 2016 militant attack in Uri, Kashmir, that India blamed on Pakistan-based groups, Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi famously declared, "Blood and water cannot flow together," and threatened to review the Indus Waters Treaty.

While India did not formally withdraw from the treaty, it accelerated work on several hydroelectric projects on the western rivers and temporarily suspended meetings of the Indus Commissioners. These actions signaled a shift toward using water as a strategic tool in the broader conflict with Pakistan.

The April 2025 suspension of the treaty following the Pahalgam attack represents an unprecedented escalation, as it marks the first time either country has formally put the agreement "in abeyance" since its signing in 1960.

Climate Change and Future Challenges

Beyond the bilateral tensions, both countries face growing challenges from climate change. The Himalayan glaciers that feed the Indus River system are melting at an accelerating rate, threatening long-term water security. Changing precipitation patterns, more frequent droughts and floods, and rising temperatures are creating new pressures on water resources.

These environmental challenges require cooperative approaches, yet the political climate has made collaboration increasingly difficult. Water experts warn that without joint management strategies, both countries could face severe water shortages in the coming decades, potentially exacerbating tensions further.

Conclusion

The history of India-Pakistan water disputes reflects the complex interplay between resource management, national security, and international relations. The Indus Waters Treaty has been remarkably resilient for over six decades, providing a framework for water sharing despite ongoing political conflicts.

However, the recent suspension of the treaty highlights its vulnerability to broader geopolitical tensions. As both countries face growing water demands and the impacts of climate change, finding sustainable solutions becomes increasingly urgent. The future of water relations between India and Pakistan will likely depend on whether they can separate water management from other conflicts and recognize their shared interest in preserving this vital resource.

The coming months will be crucial in determining whether the current crisis leads to a renegotiation of water-sharing arrangements or a deeper entrenchment of water as a tool of strategic leverage. Either way, the outcome will have profound implications for the more than 1.5 billion people who depend on the waters of the Indus basin.
