The Role of Water in South Asian Geopolitics

Introduction

In South Asia, water is not merely a natural resource; it is a strategic asset that shapes national security calculations, influences diplomatic relations, and affects the livelihoods of nearly two billion people. The region's major river systems—the Indus, Ganges, Brahmaputra, and their tributaries—transcend national boundaries, creating complex interdependencies and potential flashpoints for conflict.

The recent suspension of the Indus Waters Treaty by India following the Pahalgam attack in April 2025 has brought water issues to the forefront of regional politics once again. This development underscores how water resources can become entangled with broader security concerns and nationalist sentiments, potentially transforming rivers from sources of life to instruments of coercion.

This article examines the multifaceted role of water in South Asian geopolitics, exploring how transboundary rivers influence interstate relations, how climate change is reshaping water security calculations, and whether water will be a source of conflict or cooperation in the region's future.

Hydro-Geography of South Asia

South Asia's hydro-geography is characterized by asymmetric interdependence. The Himalayan mountain range serves as the source of the region's major rivers, giving upstream countries potential control over downstream water flows. This geographical reality creates inherent power imbalances in water relations.

The Indus River system, which includes the Indus, Jhelum, Chenab, Ravi, Beas, and Sutlej rivers, flows from Tibet and India into Pakistan. The Ganges-Brahmaputra-Meghna basin, one of the world's largest, connects Nepal, India, Bangladesh, and to some extent, China. Smaller river systems like the Kabul River (Afghanistan-Pakistan) and the Kosi River (Nepal-India) further complicate the regional water map.

This intricate network of rivers creates a situation where no country in the region can achieve complete water security without cooperation from its neighbors. Yet historical animosities, territorial disputes, and competing development priorities have often made such cooperation difficult to achieve.

India-Pakistan Water Relations

The water relationship between India and Pakistan is governed primarily by the Indus Waters Treaty (IWT) of 1960, which has been described as one of the world's most successful water-sharing agreements. The treaty allocates the eastern rivers (Ravi, Beas, and Sutlej) to India and the western rivers (Indus, Jhelum, and Chenab) to Pakistan, while allowing India limited use of the western rivers for non-consumptive purposes.

Despite three wars and numerous crises, the IWT remained intact for over six decades—until April 2025, when India announced its suspension following the Pahalgam attack. This unprecedented move has raised concerns about Pakistan's water security, as it relies on the Indus system for approximately 80% of its irrigated agriculture, which in turn accounts for a quarter of its GDP and employs nearly half its workforce.

Even before this suspension, tensions had been growing over India's construction of hydroelectric projects on the western rivers. Pakistan has consistently expressed concerns that these projects could give India the ability to manipulate water flows during times of conflict, while India has maintained that its projects comply with the treaty's provisions.

India-Bangladesh Water Dynamics

The water relationship between India and Bangladesh centers on the Ganges River and its tributaries. The 1996 Ganges Water Sharing Treaty governs the allocation of water at the Farakka Barrage during the dry season, but Bangladesh has often expressed dissatisfaction with the actual water flows it receives.

A more contentious issue is India's Interlinking of Rivers (ILR) project, which aims to connect major rivers through canals to transfer water from "surplus" to "deficit" basins. Bangladesh fears this project could significantly reduce water flows into its territory, affecting agriculture, fisheries, and the ecologically sensitive Sundarbans mangrove forest.

The proposed Teesta River water-sharing agreement has been another point of contention. Despite reaching a tentative agreement at the central government level, opposition from the Indian state of West Bengal has prevented its finalization, highlighting how domestic politics can complicate transboundary water management.

India-Nepal Hydropower Politics

Nepal possesses enormous hydropower potential due to its mountainous terrain and abundant water resources. However, developing this potential requires significant investment and access to markets, creating a dependency on India as both an investor and a buyer of electricity.

Several treaties govern water cooperation between India and Nepal, including the Kosi Agreement (1954), the Gandak Agreement (1959), and the Mahakali Treaty (1996). These agreements have been criticized in Nepal for allegedly favoring Indian interests, contributing to a perception that India exploits Nepal's water resources without providing adequate compensation.

Recent years have seen efforts to reset this relationship, with both countries expressing interest in developing Nepal's hydropower for mutual benefit. However, nationalist sentiments, historical grievances, and concerns about sovereignty continue to complicate these efforts.

The China Factor

China's role in South Asian water politics has grown significantly in recent years. As the upstream riparian for many of the region's major rivers, including the Brahmaputra (known as the Yarlung Tsangpo in China), China's dam-building activities and water diversion plans have raised concerns in downstream countries, particularly India and Bangladesh.

China's ambitious South-North Water Diversion Project and its construction of dams on the Yarlung Tsangpo have sparked fears about reduced water flows and increased vulnerability to flooding or drought. The lack of a water-sharing treaty between China and India adds to these concerns, as does China's general reluctance to engage in multilateral water governance frameworks.

China's water-related investments in Pakistan through the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) have added another dimension to regional water politics. These investments, which include dams and irrigation projects, have strengthened the China-Pakistan relationship while potentially increasing India's strategic concerns.

Climate Change and Water Security

Climate change is fundamentally altering water security calculations across South Asia. The Himalayan glaciers, which feed the region's major rivers, are melting at an accelerating rate, potentially leading to increased flows in the short term but severe water shortages in the long term.

Changing precipitation patterns, more frequent extreme weather events, and rising temperatures are already affecting agricultural productivity and water availability. The World Bank has warned that climate change could push more than 60 million people in South Asia into extreme poverty by 2030, with water-related impacts being a major factor.

These environmental challenges transcend national boundaries and require cooperative approaches. Yet the political climate in the region has often made such cooperation difficult, creating a dangerous disconnect between environmental realities and political responses.

Water as a Strategic Weapon

The concept of "water wars" has been debated extensively in the context of South Asia. While full-scale wars exclusively over water have not occurred, water has increasingly been used as a strategic lever in broader conflicts.

India's recent suspension of the Indus Waters Treaty represents perhaps the most explicit use of water as a strategic tool in the region's history. By threatening Pakistan's water security, India is applying pressure that could have severe economic and humanitarian consequences, potentially forcing concessions on other issues.

This approach, sometimes called "hydro-diplomacy" or "hydro-hegemony," reflects the growing securitization of water resources in the region. As water scarcity increases due to population growth, economic development, and climate change, the temptation to use water as leverage in interstate relations may grow stronger.

Potential for Regional Cooperation

Despite the challenges, there are compelling reasons for South Asian countries to cooperate on water issues. Shared vulnerabilities to climate change, the potential economic benefits of coordinated water management, and the humanitarian costs of water conflicts all provide incentives for collaboration.

The South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation (SAARC) has attempted to address water issues, but with limited success due to broader political tensions. More promising are basin-specific initiatives and bilateral agreements that focus on technical cooperation and data sharing.

International organizations and third-party mediators have also played important roles in facilitating water cooperation. The World Bank's involvement in the Indus Waters Treaty negotiations is a notable example, though its ability to enforce the treaty or resolve disputes has proven limited.

Conclusion

Water will undoubtedly remain a critical factor in South Asian geopolitics for decades to come. The region's complex hydro-geography, historical animosities, and shared vulnerabilities to climate change create both challenges and opportunities for interstate relations.

The recent suspension of the Indus Waters Treaty represents a dangerous escalation in the use of water as a strategic weapon. If this approach becomes normalized, it could undermine decades of efforts to establish rules-based water governance in the region and potentially trigger cycles of retaliation that harm all parties involved.

A more hopeful scenario would see South Asian countries recognizing their shared interests in sustainable water management and developing more robust cooperative mechanisms. This would require political leadership that prioritizes long-term water security over short-term strategic advantages—a challenging but necessary shift in a region where water is increasingly becoming a matter of national security.

As climate change intensifies and water scarcity grows, the choices made by South Asian leaders regarding transboundary waters will have profound implications for regional stability, economic development, and the well-being of nearly a quarter of humanity. The path they choose—cooperation or conflict—will shape the region's future for generations to come.
